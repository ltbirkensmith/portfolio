Project Overview

The Columbia Space Initiative is launching a 1U CubeSat satellite in April 2026, with a larger 6U satellite planned for 2027 or later. Both launches have secured funding, making this a real spaceflight mission with tangible impact. As a member of the structures subteam, I design satellite structural components, prototype deployable mechanisms, and perform thermal analysis to ensure mission success in the harsh environment of space.

My primary work focuses on the structures subteam, which builds satellite structures and considers the satellite as a whole in thermodynamic applications. This includes ensuring structural integrity during launch loads, thermal stability in orbit, and successful deployment of mechanisms once in space.

Technical Challenges

Deployable Mechanisms: Designed mechanisms that must remain securely stowed during launch vibrations and acoustic loads, then reliably deploy once in the zero-gravity environment of space. These mechanisms move photographic equipment into precise positions for mission objectives.

Thermal Management: Space presents extreme thermal challenges with temperatures varying from direct sunlight (+120°C) to deep shade (-180°C). Our thermal analysis ensures the IR spectrometer remains at its required operating temperature of -37°C through careful thermal design, insulation, and potentially active heating/cooling systems.

Regulatory Compliance: Satellite launches require extensive documentation and approval from multiple agencies. I contribute to compliance documentation ensuring our design meets FCC frequency regulations, NASA payload safety requirements, and FAA launch authorization standards.

Design Innovations

Prototype Door Design: Developed a prototype door mechanism for the CubeSat that allows controlled access to internal components while maintaining structural integrity. The door design incorporates sliding rails that enable smooth deployment of photographic equipment in the microgravity environment.

Rapid Prototyping Workflow: Established an efficient workflow using 3D printing for complex geometries and waterjet cutting for metal components. This allows rapid iteration of designs based on testing feedback and integration requirements with external partner teams.

Thermal Analysis Methodology

Using Ansys Thermal Desktop, I model the 6U satellite's thermal behavior in the space environment. This includes:

  • Solar radiation heating from direct sunlight
  • Earth's infrared radiation (albedo effect)
  • Radiative cooling to deep space
  • Internal heat generation from electronics
  • Thermal conductivity through satellite structure
  • Transient analysis during orbital eclipse transitions

The analysis ensures all components remain within operational temperature ranges, with particular attention to the IR spectrometer's -37°C requirement for optimal performance.

Collaboration & Integration

Working with CalPoly Pomona's Pleiades mission team to ensure our structural components remain compatible with evolving mission requirements. This collaboration requires regular communication, documentation updates, and design flexibility to accommodate changes from both teams while maintaining compliance with launch vehicle requirements.

Mission Significance

These CubeSat missions represent real spaceflight hardware that will be launched into orbit. The 1U mission in April 2026 serves as a technology demonstration and pathfinder for the more complex 6U mission. Success of these missions will demonstrate Columbia's capability in satellite design and operations, opening doors for future, more ambitious space projects.

The 6U satellite's IR spectrometer payload will contribute valuable scientific data once in orbit, making this not just an engineering exercise but a mission with real scientific impact.
